What a manager needs to know first

AI tools help teams move faster until something goes wrong. Then the questions are immediate: what happened, why did it happen, what was prevented, and can we prove it. Atested answers those questions without forcing teams into log archaeology or black-box guesswork.

It does this in two ways. First, governed actions are evaluated before they proceed, so many unsupported or insufficiently evidenced actions are denied before they become incidents. Second, every decision is recorded in signed, immutable records that are themselves proof when audit, review, or verification is required.

Prevent many bad actions before they land

Governed actions are checked against Atested's governance rules before they proceed. Atested resolves decisions deterministically where it can, and where deterministic resolution is not possible, it uses a structured, recorded judgment process to reach a decision. When required proof, scope, or constraints are missing, Atested can deny the action before the repo, message channel, or downstream system is affected.

Replace reconstruction with auditable facts

When something does go wrong, Atested provides signed records showing what was requested, what evidence was presented, what governance rules were applied, what decision was made, what happened next, and where the issue occurred. That reduces investigation effort and gives managers a defensible account of AI operations.

Signed records

Every decision is recorded and cryptographically signed into an immutable chain. Each record is linked so tampering breaks verification and the resulting evidence can be independently checked later.

Proof packets

Generate attestation artifacts that auditors, customers, and internal stakeholders can verify without trusting the runtime by assertion alone.

Institutional auditability

Policy evaluation, ALLOW or DENY decisions, returned guidance, signed records, and proof packets come together as a defensible audit trail. The result is not just a log of activity, but verifiable evidence of what was requested, what evidence was presented, what governance rules were applied, what decision was made, what happened next, and how that decision can be checked later.

Platform-agnostic observation hooks

Any AI tool that supports action hooks can connect to Atested's observation endpoint. Claude Code, Cursor, Cline, Windsurf, and other tools can report native operations so Atested can measure governed versus ungoverned activity across the real workflow.

Scoped approvals for opaque operations

Some governed code can't produce the evidence Atested requires — it's opaque to inspection. When that code is mission-essential and keeps getting DENY, the operator can grant a scoped approval for it. The approval is pinned to the artifact's exact content hash, deployment context, and active policy version. Change the artifact and the approval expires. The approval and any future revocation are signed into the governance chain. This means Atested never blocks you from getting work done — but it ensures the decision to allow unvetted code is explicit, scoped, and permanently recorded.

Not just proof after the fact

Atested does more than preserve evidence for later review. It can stop governed actions from proceeding when required proof, scope, or constraints are missing. In practice, that means many unsupported or hallucinated action attempts can be denied before they become operational events.

Governance transparency

Atested governs every action that flows through it. It cannot force every action to flow through it, because AI tools also have native capabilities that operate outside any governance layer. That is a structural reality in open environments.

What Atested does is make that boundary visible and measurable. Governed operations produce full signed records. Ungoverned native operations can be observed and counted through action hooks, so managers can see how much of their AI activity is actually under governance.

The result is a Transparency metric: a clear percentage showing governed operations relative to total observed AI activity. The goal is not pretending you start at 100 percent. The goal is knowing where you are and having a path to improve it.

What ALLOW and DENY actually mean

Atested does not just record an action after the fact. It evaluates the action before it proceeds. ALLOW means the action met the active policy with the required proof, scope, or constraints. DENY means the action did not meet those requirements, and the record will show what was missing.

Example ALLOW

Examples of governed actions that proceed:

  • A coding agent writes to a file inside an approved project path using an allowed tool and the required execution context.
  • An agent sends a message through an approved channel with the expected recipient scope and required supporting context.

Example DENY

Examples of governed actions that should make a team stop and look twice:

  • An agent tries to rewrite deployment files outside the approved workspace or touch production configuration it was never authorized to change.
  • An agent submits a call to Atested saying the tests passed and requesting permission to commit, along with evidence. Atested checks the evidence, finds it lacking, and DENIES the commit request, preventing the AI error from landing. The agent can then resubmit with better evidence or try something different. The user notices nothing happened, while Atested keeps an immutable record as it does for all governed interactions.
